# The History of Computer Science

<!--more-->

History that can be recorded often meets several conditions simultaneously: profound impact, verifiability, transmission, and the test of time.
Like gold refined by the sands of time—those that remain are not only important but also remarkably "fortunate."

However, another type of history does not fully meet these conditions. It was once almost universally known in a certain era, but gradually disappeared with the passage of time: no longer mentioned, no longer recorded.
This leads to a phenomenon—the so-called "generation gap": those who know no longer tell the story, and those who don't know have no way of knowing.

Computer science is a discipline where this "gap" is particularly pronounced. Its history is relatively short, but its technological evolution is extremely rapid.
Many understandings that were once considered "common sense" are quickly replaced by tools, abstractions, and new paradigms.
The purpose of this series is to attempt to uncover these forgotten "intermediate layers of history."

Looking back at the development of this discipline, a relatively clear main thread emerges:

- In the 19th century, Charles Babbage designed the Difference Engine and the Analytical Engine, and Ada Lovelace wrote the algorithm for the Analytical Engine.
- In 1936, Alan Turing proposed the Turing Machine, laying the foundation for modern computational theory.
- In 1945, ENIAC was born.
- In the same year and the following year, John von Neumann proposed the core ideas of computer architecture.
- In the 1950s, universities such as MIT, Princeton, and Berkeley established computer laboratories.
- In the 1960s, computer science became an independent discipline.
- In the 1970s, programming languages and operating systems gradually matured, and the discipline began to differentiate.

To date, computer science has gradually shown an extension in two directions:

- Downward: approaching silicon (machines)
- Upward: approaching carbon (humans)

Between these two ends lie countless layers of abstraction and encapsulation.

Most of us tend to enter this field from the end closer to "humans."
This means that to truly understand computer science, we must work backward, dismantling these encapsulations layer by layer to understand the abstractions and motivations behind them.
Many say we "stand on the shoulders of giants." From an intellectual and theoretical perspective, this is true; however, at the level of abstraction and encapsulation, I don't entirely agree.
We inherit the results, but we haven't experienced the process of derivation.

I'm a Gen Z, growing up in an era when home computers were widespread—you could say I'm a digital native. But my systematic exposure to computer science only began after entering university.
This often leads to a regret: not having fully experienced the development of computer science.

Learning a subject and forming a self-consistent structure in your mind is crucial.
If we abstract the development of computer science into a timeline, then approaching this timeline from different positions and in different ways will result in vastly different cognitive structures.
For example, if someone only understands computer science after 2010, their understanding is likely to be drastically different from someone who has been exposed to the field since the 1950s.

Take game development as an example: after the widespread adoption of commercial game engines, many people believe that the foundation of game development is the engine itself;
but before engines existed, developers had to handle low-level issues such as collision detection, resource management, and rendering processes themselves.
Modern engines have almost completely hidden these crucial but complex parts, significantly lowering the development threshold and altering developers' cognitive structures regarding this field.

This change has brought efficiency and widespread adoption, but it may also weaken the understanding of underlying mechanisms. I tend to believe that this impact is more harmful than beneficial on some levels.
A developer's way of understanding directly determines the quality of their work. The history of computer science can provide important insights in this regard.

Some argue that studying the history of computer science doesn't directly help technical skills.
This isn't entirely wrong—history won't make you write more code, nor will it directly improve engineering proficiency. But it will influence you at a deeper level: changing how you understand technology.
If you only learn existing technologies without understanding how they evolved step by step, you only grasp the conclusions, not the derivation process.
The real difference between developers often lies not in "what they know," but in whether they understand "why these things exist."

Therefore, we must look not only forward but also backward.
But looking back is not about nostalgia, but about finding the questions that were once raised and how those questions were solved step by step.
